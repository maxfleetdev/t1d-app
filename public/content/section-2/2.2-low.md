Low blood sugar, medically known as **hypoglycaemia** (or a "hypo"), occurs when the level of glucose (sugar) in your blood drops below your target range.

For people living with Type 1 Diabetes, a hypo happens when there is too much insulin in the body relative to the amount of glucose in the bloodstream.

---

## 1. What Counts as Low Blood Sugar?

In the UK, clinical guidelines (such as those from the NHS and Diabetes UK) define low blood sugar as a reading of **4.0 mmol/L or lower**. 

A helpful phrase widely used in diabetes care is: **"4 is the floor."**

---

## 2. Common Causes of Low Blood Sugar

Hypoglycaemia can occur unexpectedly due to several common factors:

* **Excess insulin:** Taking too much bolus insulin for a meal or administering an incorrect correction dose.
* **Delayed or missed meals:** Taking insulin but eating late, eating fewer carbohydrates than planned, or skipping a meal altogether.
* **Unplanned or intense exercise:** Physical activity increases insulin sensitivity and uses up glucose reserves, sometimes hours after the activity stops.
* **Alcohol consumption:** Drinking alcohol hinders the liver's ability to release stored glucose into the blood.
* **Hot weather:** Heat can cause insulin to be absorbed more quickly by the body.

---

## 3. Recognizing the Symptoms

Hypo symptoms can appear suddenly and vary from person to person. Common early warning signs include:

* Sweating or feeling clammy
* Shaking or trembling
* Rapid heartbeat (palpitations)
* Feeling hungry
* Dizziness or lightheadedness
* Irritability, mood changes, or anxiety
* Pale skin
* Tingling in the lips or tongue

If left untreated, severe hypoglycaemia can cause confusion, slurred speech, loss of consciousness, or seizures.

---

## 4. How Low Blood Sugar is Treated: The "Rule of 15"

To treat a hypo quickly and safely, follow these steps immediately:

1. **Treat with Fast-Acting Sugar:** Consume **15–20g of fast-acting carbohydrate**. Examples include:
   * 4–5 glucotabs or jelly babies
   * 150ml of fruit juice or non-diet sugary drink
   * 200ml of milk (if sugary snacks are unavailable)
2. **Wait and Test:** Wait **15 minutes**, then test your blood glucose again.
3. **Repeat if Necessary:** If your level is still below 4.0 mmol/L, consume another 15–20g of fast-acting sugar and wait 15 minutes to retest.
4. **Follow Up with Complex Carbs:** Once your blood sugar rises above 4.0 mmol/L, eat a longer-acting carbohydrate snack (e.g., a slice of toast, a biscuit, or your next scheduled meal) to keep your levels stable.

---

## 5. Severe Hypoglycaemia: Emergency Response

If a person experiencing a hypo becomes unconscious, has a seizure, or cannot swallow safely:

* **Do not** put food or liquids in their mouth.
* Place them in the recovery position if safe to do so.
* Administer emergency **Glucagon** if trained to do so.
* **Call 999 immediately** for emergency assistance.
