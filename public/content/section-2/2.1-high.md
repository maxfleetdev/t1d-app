High blood sugar, medically known as **hyperglycaemia** (or a "hyper"), occurs when the level of glucose (sugar) in your blood rises above your target range. 

For people living with Type 1 Diabetes, high blood sugar happens when there isn't enough insulin in the body to allow glucose to move out of the bloodstream and into your cells.

---

## 1. What Counts as High Blood Sugar?

Blood glucose targets can vary slightly depending on your personal care plan, but UK clinical guidelines (such as those from the NHS and NICE) generally define target blood sugar levels for adults with Type 1 Diabetes as:

* **Before meals:** 4.0 to 7.0 mmol/L
* **At least 90 minutes after meals:** 5.0 to 9.0 mmol/L
* **When sleeping / overnight:** 6.0 to 10.0 mmol/L

Generally, a blood sugar reading **above 10.0 mmol/L** is considered high (hyperglycaemia) and requires attention, though an occasional mild elevation after eating high-carb foods can happen.

---

## 2. Common Causes of High Blood Sugar

Hyperglycaemia can be triggered by several everyday factors:

* **Insulin mismatch:** Missing a dose, underestimating carbohydrates, or taking too little bolus insulin.
* **Illness or infection:** Colds, flu, and infections trigger stress hormones that raise blood sugar.
* **Stress or anxiety:** Physical or emotional stress causes the body to release cortisol and adrenaline.
* **Equipment issues:** A blocked infusion set on an insulin pump, expired insulin, or insulin exposed to heat/freezing temperatures.
* **Inactivity:** Less physical activity than usual.

---

## 3. Recognizing the Symptoms

Symptoms of high blood sugar usually develop slowly over several hours or days. They include:

* Feeling excessively thirsty
* Needing to urinate more frequently than usual (especially at night)
* Extreme tiredness or fatigue
* Blurred vision
* Headaches
* Dry mouth

---

## 4. How High Blood Sugar is Treated

When your blood sugar reading is high, take the following steps to bring it back into target safely:

1. **Administer a Correction Dose:** Use your rapid-acting (bolus) insulin as calculated by your insulin-to-carb ratio or correction factor (sensitivity factor).
2. **Drink Plenty of Water:** Hydration helps your kidneys flush excess glucose out through your urine.
3. **Check for Ketones:** If your blood sugar is **above 13.0–15.0 mmol/L**, check your blood or urine for ketones.
4. **Identify the Cause:** Check your insulin pump tubing/cannula if applicable, or ensure your insulin pen/cartridge hasn't spoiled.

> **Important:** Do not perform intense exercise if your blood sugar is high and ketones are present, as exercise can cause ketone levels to rise further.

---

## 5. When High Blood Sugar Becomes an Emergency: DKA

If high blood sugar is left untreated and the body completely lacks insulin, it begins breaking down fat for energy. This process produces toxic acids called **ketones**, leading to a life-threatening condition called **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)**.

**Seek immediate emergency medical care (call 999 or go to A&E) if you experience:**
* Moderate to high levels of blood or urine ketones
* Vomiting or inability to keep fluids down
* Abdominal pain
* Rapid, deep breathing
* Fruity-smelling breath
* Confusion or drowsiness
