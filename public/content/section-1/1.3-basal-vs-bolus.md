As discussed, Type 1 Diabetics no longer produces their own insulin. To stay healthy, they must replace insulin manually.  
Because a healthy human body releases insulin in two distinct ways—a steady background drip and sudden bursts during meals—insulin therapy uses two main types of insulin to mirror that balance: Basal and Bolus.

### Basal Insulin: Long acting
Even when you are not eating—like when you are sleeping, working, or watching TV—your body still needs energy. Your liver constantly releases small amounts of stored sugar into your blood to keep your brain and organs functioning.
Basal insulin handles this background sugar.  

- What it does: Keeps your blood sugar steady between meals and overnight.  
- How fast it works: It is long-acting. It releases slowly and evenly into your body over 12 to 24 hours (or more), without a sharp spike in action.  
- When you take it: Usually once or twice a day at the same time every day. (If you use an insulin pump, the pump continuously delivers tiny amounts of rapid-acting insulin every few minutes to act as your basal).  

### Bolus Insulin: Rapid acting
Whenever you eat foods that contain carbohydrates (like bread, pasta, fruit, or rice), your body breaks them down into glucose. This causes your blood sugar to rise quickly. Bolus insulin is designed to catch that spike.
- What it does: Covers the carbohydrates in your food so your cells can use the sugar for energy. It is also used as a "correction dose" to bring your blood sugar down if it drifts too high.  
- How fast it works: It is rapid-acting or short-acting. It starts working within 5 to 15 minutes, peaks around 1 to 2 hours later, and leaves your system after 3 to 5 hours.  
- When you take it: Right before or during meals, or whenever your blood sugar is above your target range.  

### Why You Need Both
Managing Type 1 Diabetes requires a continuous balance between both types:
- Without Basal insulin: Your blood sugar would slowly climb higher and higher between meals and overnight, eventually leading to dangerous complications like Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA).
- Without Bolus insulin: Every time you ate carbohydrates, your blood sugar would spike out of target range because there wouldn't be enough fast-acting insulin to handle the influx of food.  

Together, basal and bolus insulin act as a team to replicate what a working pancreas does automatically, keeping blood sugar stable around the clock.
